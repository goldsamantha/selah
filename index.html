Selah

She believes first and foremost in herself. Selah is 17 years old, captain of the Spirit Squad, most likely candidate for class valedictorian, and at the heart of it: ruthlessly selfish and as cruel as circumstances call for with little regard for consequences.

She’s never made a habit out of feeling guilty.

Paloma

She's a sophomore, and new to Pontomic High.

Her camera is never far from her hands, and her hair tends to cover her face. An aura of uncertainty follows her, but she’s sharp. She’s quick. She’s got an eye for things.

Paloma wants to belong somewhere, to feel grounded, to have a home away from home. And luckily, Selah needs someone to take over the Spades after she graduates.

My favorite book, since the first time I read it in fifth grade, is a tragically under- hyped piece of fiction written by Fran Ross, Oreo. It’s this modern day odyssey of a biracial— black mom, white dad—teenage girl whose strengths are in her multiplicity: her willingness to be this and that and then that too and this here and that over there, while never sacrificing one identity for the other.

That book has always influenced the characters I’m drawn to create: girls of color who are unapologetic in their volume, or their attitude. Who seek not to make themselves easier to understand, because they already understand themselves, and if you’re in the know, then you understand them too.

As is strongly evident in SELAH AND THE SPADES, young women, and the roles placed upon them, are the core of this story. I have no patience for a lack of diversity in storytelling, nor am I interested in continuing the system of “othering” the “other.”

Selah and Paloma are two girls from outside of Philly who are not made to feel ashamed of who they are. They have bad days, they do not smile when you tell them to, they happily manipulate the world to their advantage. They are unapologetically multitudinous in their being.

This film seeks to remind young girls of color that they can choose how to define themselves, and they can shape their own worlds—and if they don’t want to, they never have to apologize for it.
