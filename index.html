Selah Madewell believes first and foremost in herself. She’s 17 years old, captain of the Spirit Squad, most likely candidate for class valedictorian, and at the heart of it: ruthlessly selfish and as cruel as circumstances call for with no regard for consequences.

She’s never made a habit out of feeling guilty.

This is the story of how she loses that control.
As is strongly evident in SELAH AND THE SPADES, young women, and the roles placed upon them, are at the forefront of this story. I have no patience for a lack of diversity. I am not interested in continuing the system of “othering” the “other.” Teenage girls, especially girls of color, are so rarely given the opportunity to tell the everyday mumblecore stories that we experience, because we must busy ourselves with telling the world of our various struggles. I am not interested in that; we are so much more than we have been allowed to show, we are unapologetically multitudinous in our being. We all know that representation matters, this cannot be denied. In a society where media creates so much of how we feel about others, and ourselves, it matters absolutely. How o en does a black girl get the chance, in ction, to be so unashamedly wicked, without having to defend her race, her hair, her clothes, or any other super cial thing about herself? How o en is a woman of color given a chance to be the anti-hero? To de ne herself?
Our definitions of good and evil are challenged these days in popular media, where the lines of justice are drawn in spirals and dropped at random amongst characters with questionable morals. Yet, even in this intriguing landscape, people of color are still tied down to the same stories and the too tired tropes. It’s boring.
This project seeks to contribute to the world of black girls one of our own who isn’t made to feel ashamed of who she is, who has bad days, who does not smile, who unapologetically manipulates the world to her advantage. is lm seeks to show young girls of color that they can choose how to de ne themselves, and they can shape their world—it does not seek to tell them for what side.
